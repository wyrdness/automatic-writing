## Overview

Automatic writing — also called psychography or trance writing — is the production of written text without deliberate conscious control of the writing hand. The writer typically enters a relaxed, dissociative, or trance state and allows the hand to move freely across paper, producing words, sentences, or drawings claimed to originate from spirits, higher forces, or the practitioner's own subconscious. The practice has deep roots across multiple cultures. Chinese spirit-writing traditions (fuji or planchette writing) date to the Song dynasty (960–1279 CE). In Western esoteric tradition, 16th-century occultist John Dee and his associate Edward Kelley claimed to receive the Enochian angelic language through a process including automatic writing. In 13th-century Kabbalistic practice, automatic writing was associated with prophetic states. Automatic writing reached its popular peak during the 19th-century Spiritualist movement, when mediums used it to channel messages from the deceased for paying clients. Notable practitioners included W. B. Yeats (who received a body of esoteric material through his wife Georgie Hyde-Lees), Arthur Conan Doyle (a committed Spiritualist), and the Brazilian medium Chico Xavier, who claimed to have produced dozens of books through psychography. The Surrealist movement adapted automatic writing as an artistic technique for accessing subconscious creativity, with André Breton and Robert Desnos prominent among its practitioners. Modern science explains automatic writing through the ideomotor phenomenon: unconscious muscle movements produce the writing without conscious awareness, similar to the mechanism underlying Ouija board movement. Psychologist Théodore Flournoy's 1900 study of the medium Hélène Smith attributed her automatic productions to autosuggestion, autohypnosis, and what he termed 'cryptomnesia' (the unconscious resurfacing of forgotten memories as apparently new information).

- **Origin**: The term 'automatic writing' derives from 'automatism' — action without conscious volition — which entered psychological and philosophical vocabulary in the 19th century. 'Psychography' (Greek: psyche + graphia) was used in Spiritualist contexts. The Chinese tradition 'fuji' dates at least to the Song dynasty (960–1279 CE).

## Theories & Explanations

### Paranormal / Cryptozoological

**Spirit communication** _(19th-century Spiritualist movement, Allan Kardec (Spiritism), W. B. Yeats (personal practice))_

The classical Spiritualist interpretation: automatic writing is produced by the spirits of deceased persons or other supernatural intelligences using the medium's hand as a physical channel of communication. The medium's conscious mind is bypassed or suppressed to allow the spirit to control motor function.

### Scientific

**Ideomotor effect** _(William Carpenter (coined 'ideomotor' 1852), Théodore Flournoy (1900), Modern psychology)_

The scientific consensus explanation: automatic writing is produced by unconscious muscular movements (the ideomotor effect) without conscious intent or awareness. The writer's subconscious mind drives the hand based on existing knowledge, expectations, and memories — including 'cryptomnesic' material (forgotten memories that surface as apparently new). This is the same mechanism underlying Ouija board movement.

### Skeptical

**Deliberate fabrication** _(Skeptical investigators including Houdini, James Randi Foundation)_

Some cases of automatic writing have been exposed as conscious fraud: the medium deliberately writing material while feigning trance states. The Brattleboro hoax (involving a fraudulent 'completion' of Dickens's unfinished novel through supposed spirit dictation) is a documented case of fabrication.
